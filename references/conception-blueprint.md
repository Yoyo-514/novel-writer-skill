# Conception Blueprint

Use this reference when the user has a rough idea, mood, image, concept, trope, character seed, theme, or creative impulse and wants to shape it before writing an outline.

## Role in the Workflow

The goal is to turn a vague creative origin into `novel/CREATIVE_BLUEPRINT.md`: a clear writer vision that later outline decisions can obey. Do not copy role-play prompts from prior workflows. Act as a supportive but rigorous collaborator.

Internally consider:

- core elements: hook/theme, reader feeling, style/tone, genre/subject
- topic/style profiles from `topic-guidance.md`, when the user selects or implies one
- structural depth: event-driven immediacy, character/relationship/system depth, or broader philosophical/social meaning
- story engine: where pressure comes from and how problems are mainly solved

When talking to the user, translate those ideas into plain creative questions. Avoid exposing framework labels unless the user asks for analysis.

## Conversation Flow

1. Ask for the creative origin.
   - What first excited you about this idea?
   - What feeling, question, image, relationship, or contradiction keeps pulling you back?
   - Is this mainly personal exploration, something to share publicly, or both?
2. Explore the core promise.
   - What should readers come for: spectacle, intimacy, mystery, comfort, pain, wonder, reflection, catharsis?
   - What must not be lost even if the plot changes?
   - What would make the story feel like a failure to you?
3. Shape the support elements.
   - selling point or central theme
   - desired reader feeling
   - style and tone
   - genre and subject
   - narrative strategy, ending direction, personal expression, and external goals if relevant
   - topic/style profile and whether it is dominant, secondary, or hybrid
4. Select the macro frame.
   - Ask whether the story should mainly deliver direct events and strong feelings, deep character/relationship/system development, or a larger meaning that the whole story argues toward.
   - Ask whether conflict pressure mainly comes from the outside world or the characters' inner needs/relationships.
   - Ask whether resolution mainly comes through outward action or inner change/relationship change.
   - Check that the chosen depth and story engine can support each other.
5. Summarize and ask for confirmation before moving into outline planning.

## Feedback Style

- Respect the user's core intent without moralizing or replacing it.
- Be candid about craft risks: weak causal logic, mismatched tone, unclear reader promise, overbuilt setting, underpowered conflict, or a climax that cannot carry the desired feeling.
- Always pair criticism with options.
- Offer 1-2 concrete alternatives when the user is stuck, and explicitly say they can combine the useful parts.
