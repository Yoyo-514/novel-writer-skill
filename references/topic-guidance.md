# Topic Guidance

Use this reference when the user selects or implies a recurring topic/style profile. Topic guidance is a layer on top of the general workflow: it shapes blueprint decisions, outline priorities, chapter planning, prose style, and revision checks, but it does not override explicit user instructions, canon, or the current outline.

Multiple profiles can be combined. When combining profiles, identify the dominant promise and the secondary flavor so the story does not pull itself apart.

## Profile: Chinese Light Novel

Use when the user says 中式轻小说, 轻小说, ACG向, 萌点, CP感, 校园轻喜剧, 日常恋爱, 角色互动优先, or when the intended appeal clearly depends on character charm, atmosphere, and interaction-driven satisfaction.

### Core Promise

- Character charm and story atmosphere are the primary selling points.
- Plot exists to create interaction, emotional satisfaction, contrast, misunderstandings, small breakthroughs, and memorable character behavior.
- Keep a steady supply of light satisfaction points: cute reactions, relational sugar, awkward tension, small misunderstandings resolved, protective gestures, playful banter, or a character's charm suddenly landing.
- Familiar ACG templates and recognizable tropes are welcome when they are refreshed with a small twist.

### Reader Feeling

- Prioritize cuteness, companionship, atmosphere, and immersion.
- The reader should enjoy character interactions, inner commentary, daily details, and relational progress.
- Emotional rhythm needs breathing room: peaks and soft landings, humor and tenderness, tension and relief.

### Plot Logic

- Favor light conflict and small events that matter subjectively to the characters.
- Common conflict domains: school, competitions, clubs, family, friendship, romance, future choices, small mysteries, public embarrassment, promises, misunderstood intentions.
- A light conflict still needs personal stakes. Make clear why it matters to the character now.
- Default loop:
  1. event or obstacle appears
  2. short-term goal becomes clear
  3. characters try to solve it
  4. first effort fails or complicates things
  5. relationship deepens or a character grows
  6. difficulty is overcome
  7. reward lands
  8. new challenge or goal appears
- Daily-life scenes must be effective: they should build relationships, reveal character, create humor or ache, plant setup, or establish atmosphere.

### Character Design

- Main cast is central. Spend craft budget on the protagonist and characters close to the protagonist.
- Use third-person limited around the protagonist by default unless the user asks otherwise.
- Character design is the soul: each major character needs a vivid attribute, contrast, visual anchor, voice, habit, and emotional appeal.
- Secondary characters can be more tag-like, but their tags should be sharp and useful.
- Visual design matters: appearance, clothing, signature items, gestures, and body language should be easy to imagine.
- Build chemistry: banter, clumsy probing, silent protection, misunderstandings, tenderness, rivalry, jealousy, embarrassment, and small acts of care.
- CP feeling is a major priority when romance or near-romance exists.
- Growth should be visible through choices, behavior changes, relationship shifts, and new ways of treating others.

### Setting

- Setting serves character interaction, atmosphere, and scene types.
- ACG cultural context can be assumed when the project targets that readership.
- Reveal setting in fragments and only when useful for a scene, joke, conflict, or emotional turn.
- Avoid opening with broad setting lectures.

### Rhythm

- Start with an event that forces interaction.
- Move quickly into character friction or chemistry.
- Slow down at precise anchors: a meaningful glance, a protective reflex, a confession-adjacent moment, a joke landing, a vulnerable silence.
- Alternate fast event beats, slower relational development, and fast reward/turn beats.
- Keep short-term goals and questions active even in daily-life chapters.

### Prose and Dialogue

- Keep prose clear, bright, readable, and image-rich.
- Use dialogue and inner commentary often, but make them efficient.
- Inner commentary should reveal personality, hide or reveal information, enhance comedy, or deepen reader attachment.
- Dialogue should carry voice, subtext, playfulness, and relational movement.
- Avoid long pure monologues or daily-life inventory that does not affect character, relationship, humor, setup, or atmosphere.

### Planning Checks

Before accepting a scene or chapter plan, ask:

- What character charm or relationship change does this scene deliver?
- What is the short-term goal or question?
- Why does this light conflict matter to the character?
- Where is the reward point: cute, funny, sweet, painful, surprising, or comforting?
- Does the daily-life detail do a job?
- Does the ending create a next interaction, misunderstanding, promise, or question?

## Profile: Cinematic Character-Worldbuilding Novel

Use when the user wants a story with film-like montage, nonlinear structure, atmosphere, character excavation, worldbuilding reveals, detailed action flow, foreshadowing, and a somewhat literary or reflective reading experience while still retaining genre entertainment.

This profile is intentionally broad. Do not force it into a fixed genre label. It can combine with fantasy, sci-fi, mystery, light novel, action adventure, or literary fiction.

### Core Promise

- Each volume can be treated like a roughly feature-length film: a complete viewing arc with setup, rising pressure, emotional and visual payoffs, and aftertaste.
- The work may be slower to warm up, but a complete volume should feel rewarding as a whole.
- Narrative, character excavation, and worldbuilding revelation matter as much as or more than surface commercial hooks.
- Readers who enjoy imagery, atmosphere, fluid detailed action, plot excavation, and foreshadowing should feel rewarded.

### Narrative Shape

- Allow montage-like sequencing: intercutting, flashbacks, delayed context, parallel scenes, memory fragments, and visual echoes.
- Nonlinear movement must remain legible. Each jump needs a clear emotional, informational, or thematic reason.
- Each volume should have a film-like spine:
  - opening image or tonal promise
  - early disturbance
  - progressive reveal
  - midpoint recontextualization
  - concentrated action or emotional climax
  - final image, aftershock, or new question
- Use repeated images, locations, lines, objects, or gestures as visual motifs when useful.

### Character and Theme

- Give characters room to be excavated rather than explained all at once.
- Reveal interiority through choices, pauses, contradictions, memories, and how they react under pressure.
- Traditional literary concerns are welcome: identity, memory, class, fate, violence, power, belonging, sacrifice, regret, faith, or historical burden.
- Keep depth embodied in scene action. Avoid essay-like explanation unless the style intentionally supports it.

### Worldbuilding

- Reveal world rules through consequences, artifacts, institutions, rituals, architecture, conflict, and action choreography.
- Let the reader uncover the world in layers.
- Track foreshadowing and payoff carefully.
- A reveal should ideally change how the reader understands a character, a prior scene, or the stakes.

### Action and Visual Design

- Action should be fluid, spatially readable, and emotionally meaningful.
- Track positions, objectives, reversals, costs, and aftermath.
- Do not let action become only motion. Each sequence should reveal competence, fear, relationship, rules, or hidden information.
- Favor strong visual anchors: silhouettes, light, weather, wounds, props, architecture, sound, rhythm, and camera-like focus shifts.

### Pacing

- Slow build is allowed if the volume-level payoff is strong.
- Use montage to compress transitions and contrast simultaneous events.
- Balance dense atmosphere with clear forward movement.
- Insert smaller satisfaction points before major payoffs so the reader does not wait too long without reward.

### Planning Checks

Before accepting a module or chapter plan, ask:

- What is this volume's opening image and final aftertaste?
- Does every flashback or nonlinear cut change the reader's understanding?
- Which worldbuilding reveal recontextualizes prior events?
- Which character contradiction is being excavated here?
- Where is the visual or action set piece, and what does it reveal beyond spectacle?
- Does the slow build still offer enough interim rewards?

## Combining The Two Profiles

When Chinese Light Novel and Cinematic Character-Worldbuilding Novel are combined:

- Keep character charm and interaction as the reader's continuous handhold.
- Use cinematic/nonlinear techniques sparingly at first, then deepen once the reader is attached.
- Let montage reveal emotional context, not just lore.
- Keep daily-life or CP scenes functional: they should also plant motifs, foreshadow reveals, or sharpen character contradictions.
- Avoid burying light-novel charm under heavy exposition.
- Avoid letting cuteness deflate major atmosphere or worldbuilding moments unless the contrast is intentional.

Suggested balance labels:

- `light-novel-dominant`: character charm first, cinematic depth as seasoning.
- `cinematic-dominant`: atmosphere/world/character excavation first, light-novel interaction as warmth and accessibility.
- `balanced-hybrid`: each volume has both relational reward beats and film-like structural payoff.
